Aspirin? Do I really need that?

So, your doctor just prescribed you a daily “baby Aspirin” (81mg dose of Aspirin) that he wants you to take every day. Is that really necessary? What do doctors know anyway?

The truth of the matter is that you probably do.

New Studies Show a Higher Risk for Heart Attacks in HIV Positive Patients

Recent studies, consisting of over 80,000 veterans, have shown that patients infected with HIV are at a higher risk of having heart attacks.

Lowering Cardiovascular Risk through Diet and Exercise

Recent studies looked into the benefit of naturopathic treatments combined with usual care in individuals at a high risk for cardiovascular disease. Treatment plans that included a component of lifestyle change: dietary, exercise, and supplements, in addition to usual medical treatments were shown to reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease in patients.

Cholesterol and Eating Behaviors in Childhood

Heart disease (cardiovascular disease) is the number one leading cause of death in America. In 2010, this was responsible for 597,689 deaths in the United States.

A recent study published in the Canadian Medical Association Journal (CMAJ) studied 1,076 children in pre-kindergarten, between the ages of three and five, and compared their cholesterol levels with their eating behaviors.

Preventive PCI Reduces Risk of Death from Cardiac Causes

Rigorous statistical analysis showed that preventive PCI in vessels that were not occluded, but had early signs of occlusion, dramatically reduced a patient’s risk of future heart attack.

For Patients with Mechanical Heart Valves, Warfarin Remains the Better Choice

Dabigatran has been shown to be an appropriate alternative to warfarin in patients with non-valvular atrial fibrillation. Due to this fact, investigators sought to determine if dabigatran (brand name: Pradaxa) could be used as an effective alternative to warfarin in patients with mechanical heart valves. This was the premise of the RE-ALIGN trial.

Alogliptin Use in Patients with High Cardiovascular Risk

The antidiabetic drug, alogliptin, has NOT been shown to increase or decrease the risk of cardiovascular death, heart attack or stroke in patients, with history of type 2 diabetes and recent heart attack. If you have type 2 diabetes, speak to your doctor about alogliptin and other alternatives for blood sugar control.

New Diabetes Medicine Shows No Benefit for Cardiovascular Health

Saxagliptin is a new diabetes drug that works by slowing the destruction of chemicals that help the body release more insulin. With more of these chemicals, there are increased levels of insulin, and so the body is better able to absorb glucose. A recent study evaluated this medication in terms of its effect on heart health (2).

Edoxaban Not Inferior to Warfarin

This time a new study compares warfarin with edoxaban in their use for atrial fibrillation, which is a heart arrhythmia (abnormal rhythm) caused by disorganized electrical activity within the heart. Edoxaban works similarly to low molecular weight heparin or “lovenox” in that both inhibit the same protein involved in clot formation.

Use of Genetics to Dose Warfarin

Medications are processed (or metabolized) by proteins in our bodies, inactivating the medications after a period of time. Some individuals process specific medications fast, while others process them at a slower rate. Our genes are one of the major factors that determine how fast we metabolize medications. Researchers have been able to identify the genes involved in Warfarin activity, which are known as CYP2CP and VKORC1.

LVAD's Carry both Risks and Rewards

More aggressive treatment may come in the form of a left ventricular assist device (LVAD), which is a mechanical circulatory device that is used to aid a failing heart in pumping blood.Used mostly in severe cases of heart failure, LVAD’s have proven to successfully manage those with serious heart failure and relieve many of its symptoms.

Mitral Valve Repair or Replacement: Which Has Better Outcomes for Heart Disease?

The study looked at 251 patients who had mitral regurgitation and required surgery to fix the problem. The patients were randomly assigned to have either a repair or a replacement, and then their progress was tracked for 12 months. The researchers looked at the strength of the patient’s left ventricle, the mortality rates, the rates of heart attacks, and the return of mitral regurgitation to measure how well the patient’s had done.
